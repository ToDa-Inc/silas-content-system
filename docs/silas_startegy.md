What Makes a Good Reel
Instagram reels that perform well usually follow a clear structure.
 The goal is always the same:
Stop the scroll


Create curiosity


Deliver value


Trigger interaction


There are two main formats that work especially well for Conny’s niche.

1️⃣ Short-Form Reels (≈ 7 Seconds)
Short reels are designed to stop the scroll and trigger curiosity immediately.
 They are simple, punchy, and highly shareable.
Structure
1. Ultra Clear, Catchy Hook (first 1–2 seconds)
 The viewer must instantly understand the topic and feel curious.
Examples:
“Smart employees notice these red flags early.”


“If your boss says this, be careful.”


“Most professionals communicate wrong.”


The hook should feel direct, specific, and slightly provocative.

2. Matching Sound / Audio
The audio should support the message and emotional tone.
Good sounds for this format:
trending sounds


subtle background beats


dramatic or tension-building sounds


The sound should enhance attention, not distract from the message.

3. Curiosity Trigger
The reel should hint at an insight without explaining everything.
The goal is to make the viewer think:
“I need to know more.”
Example:
Text on screen:
“High performers often stay quiet in meetings.
 There is a reason for that.”

4. Value in the Caption
Since the reel is very short, the real value is delivered in the caption.
The caption should provide:
explanation


insight


frameworks


examples


This encourages viewers to read, save, and comment.

5. Clear Call-to-Action
End the caption with a simple CTA that triggers engagement.
Examples:
“Comment ‘Clarity’ if you want to communicate like this.”


“Have you experienced this at work?”


“Save this if this situation feels familiar.”



2️⃣ Talking Head Reels (≈ 60 Seconds)
Talking head reels allow deeper explanation and build authority and trust.
They work extremely well for topics such as:
leadership communication


workplace dynamics


psychological insights


practical communication frameworks



Structure
1. Strong Hook (first 3 seconds)
The hook must immediately identify the situation or problem.
Examples:
“Most professionals communicate wrong in difficult conversations.”


“Here are three sentences that instantly calm heated discussions.”


“Smart employees notice these workplace red flags early.”


The hook should create:
curiosity


recognition


emotional relevance



2. Context (seconds 3–10)
Briefly describe the situation.
Example:
“You’re trusted with important work…
 but somehow you're never invited to the decision-making meetings.”
This creates relatability.
The viewer should think:
“That happens to me.”

3. Insight / Lesson (seconds 10–45)
This is the core value of the reel.
Provide:
explanations


communication examples


frameworks


psychology insights


Example structure:
Point 1


Point 2


Point 3


Keep it clear, structured, and easy to understand.

4. Simple Conclusion (seconds 45–55)
Summarize the key insight.
Example:
“Execution without influence is not growth.
 It’s a ceiling.”
A strong conclusion makes the message memorable and shareable.

5. Clear Call-to-Action (final seconds)
Encourage the viewer to take the next step.
Examples:
“Comment ‘Clarity’ if you want to communicate like this.”


“Follow for more communication insights.”


“Join my free live training if you want to master difficult conversations.”

4️⃣ What Breaks a Reel
❌ Weak hook
Example
“Today I want to talk about communication.”
Too slow.

❌ Too abstract
Example
“Communication is important.”
No concrete situation.

❌ No emotional trigger
If viewers feel nothing:
no comments
no shares

❌ Too much explanation
Reels should be simple insights, not lectures.

❌ No relatability
People must recognize themselves.
Example
Bad
“Corporate environments require…”
Good
“You’re trusted with work… but not invited to the meeting.”
